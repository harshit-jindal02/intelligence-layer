This is a great problem to think through. Since you mentioned previous solutions didn't satisfy you, let me approach this differently — starting from what actually works in practice rather than generic "plug in an ML model" advice.

The core challenge with an intelligence layer like predictAssist is that most approaches either end up being glorified dashboards (reactive, not predictive) or require so much labeled training data that they never get off the ground. Here's an architecture that avoids both traps.

The fundamental insight: You don't need to predict specific errors. You need to detect deviations from normal behavior and correlate them with known incident patterns. This is much more achievable and gives you proactive power without needing a massive labeled dataset upfront.

Phase 1 — Build a "Normal Behavior Baseline"

Ingest your historical logs and metrics into a time-series store (something like ClickHouse, TimescaleDB, or even OpenSearch). The key step most people skip: don't just store raw data. Compute behavioral features on ingestion — things like error rate per service per 5-minute window, latency percentile shifts, log volume velocity (is logging accelerating or decelerating), ratio of warn/error to info logs, and request pattern distributions. These derived signals are far more predictive than raw metrics. You then build statistical baselines per feature using your historical data — simple approaches like rolling percentiles and seasonal decomposition (STL) work surprisingly well here.

Phase 2 — Anomaly Detection Without Labels

This is where most "ML solutions" go wrong — they try supervised learning when you don't have clean labels. Instead, use unsupervised approaches. Isolation Forest and DBSCAN work well for point anomalies in your feature space. For temporal patterns, Prophet or ARIMA-based forecasting on your derived features lets you flag when a metric is deviating from its expected trajectory before it crosses a hard threshold. The key differentiator: correlate anomalies across signals. A single metric spiking is noise. Three related metrics shifting together (say, GC pause time increasing + response latency climbing + throughput dropping) is a pattern — and patterns are predictive.

Phase 3 — Incident Fingerprinting (this is the real intelligence)

This is the part that actually makes it "predictive" rather than just "anomaly detection." Take your historical incidents (outages, degradations, bugs that hit production) and work backwards: what did the logs and metrics look like in the 30-60 minutes before each incident? Extract the sequence of anomalies that preceded each one. These become your incident fingerprints. Now, when your anomaly detector fires in real-time, you pattern-match against known fingerprints. "We're seeing the same sequence of anomalies that preceded the database connection pool exhaustion incident on March 15th" — that's genuinely proactive.

Phase 4 — LLM-Powered Reasoning Layer

This is where you can add real differentiation. Use an LLM (via RAG over your incident history and runbooks) to do two things: first, when anomalies are detected, have the LLM analyze the surrounding log context and produce a natural-language explanation of what's happening and why it might be concerning. Second, have it suggest remediation actions based on what worked for similar past incidents. This turns raw anomaly alerts into actionable intelligence.

Practically, the tech stack could look like:

Your application emits logs and metrics, which flow through a collection layer (OpenTelemetry Collector is ideal since you're already in the observability space with TraceAssist). That feeds into both a time-series store for metrics and a log store. A feature computation service runs continuously, computing your behavioral features and writing them to the time-series store. An anomaly detection service reads those features, runs your detection algorithms, and emits anomaly events. A correlation engine matches anomaly patterns against your incident fingerprint library. And the LLM reasoning layer sits on top, providing the "so what?" interpretation.

What makes this different from what you've probably seen before:

The key is that each phase delivers value independently. Phase 1 alone gives you better observability than most teams have. Phase 2 catches issues that static thresholds miss. Phase 3 is where you get genuine prediction. Phase 4 makes it accessible to anyone on the team, not just the person who built it. You don't need to build all four before shipping — each layer compounds on the previous one.

The cold-start problem: You need historical data, but you also need incidents to fingerprint. Start by manually labeling your last 10-20 significant incidents and mapping them to the log/metric patterns that preceded them. That's enough to bootstrap Phase 3. The system gets smarter as it accumulates more incident data over time.

Here's what the document covers and the key design decisions:

The core architecture is a pipeline: Ingestion → Feature Engine → Anomaly Detector → Correlator → LLM Reasoner. Each stage is a separate Go package with clean interfaces, so you can build and test them independently.

The integration design is the most important part for your future vision. Everything communicates via two protocols — OTLP for telemetry data and NATS for events. PredictAssist defines a TelemetrySource interface, so when TraceAssist comes online later, you just add a new implementation without touching any downstream code. Similarly, toilAssist just subscribes to NATS subjects like prediction.incident.predicted — PredictAssist doesn't need to know toilAssist exists.

ShopSim is the sample app — a small Go microservices e-commerce system with built-in chaos endpoints. Each chaos mode (memory leak, cascade failure, DB pool exhaustion, etc.) produces a distinct telemetry fingerprint that PredictAssist learns to recognize. This gives you a controlled way to test predictions.

The 6-sprint roadmap starts with getting data flowing (ShopSim + Ingestion) before building any intelligence. Sprint 1-2 gets telemetry into ClickHouse. Sprint 3-4 adds detection and fingerprinting. Sprint 5-6 adds LLM reasoning and polishes the API.
